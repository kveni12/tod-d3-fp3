Final Project POC

TOD works for affordability only when lower-income households can still claim the new housing.

This proof of concept studies who transit-oriented development affects through income. The core argument is simple: today's TOD pattern often still looks like gentrification pressure, because development near transit is increasing faster than protected affordability. The policy answer is not less TOD. It is more affordability inside TOD, at the same time as growth.

Main question

Are municipalities with more households under $125k actually getting new transit-oriented housing, and when they do, is enough of it affordable?

Main interaction

Click municipalities to link the scatter, map, and detail views.

Why income

Income is the most direct way to ask whether TOD expands access or simply makes high-opportunity places easier for higher-income households to capture.

The Argument

Imagine a household earning around $90,000 in an MBTA-served municipality. It is not a low-income household, but it is still highly exposed to rent increases and rising entry costs. If TOD adds housing near transit but that housing is mostly market-rate, then the likely outcome is higher-income capture, not reduced displacement pressure.

1. Vulnerability

Start with the share of households under $125k. That is the exposure side of the affordability story.

2. Growth

Then ask where new units are landing over time and whether they are actually inside the TOD threshold.

3. Affordability

Finally, ask whether that growth carries enough affordable housing to counter displacement pressure.

Education and mobility belong in this story too. Rising education levels often travel with rising incomes, so they help show whether TOD is concentrating opportunity among more advantaged households. Mobility outcomes matter for the same reason: even if transit access improves and driving falls, affordability pressure can still leave lower-income households worse off if they cannot remain near that access. In other words, education and mobility help explain who is positioned to benefit from TOD, but income remains the clearest starting point.

Pressure is concentrating in a small set of municipalities

Most new housing is still market-rate

This yearly composition chart shows whether added housing is protected affordable housing or mostly market-rate production. If market-rate dominates year after year, TOD growth is less likely to reduce displacement pressure on its own.

Higher-vulnerability municipalities do not consistently capture growth

Bottom line

The tract-level evidence sharpens the same takeaway as the municipal dashboard: TOD is only anti-gentrification policy when affordability grows with it.

Resources

This is one focused D3 proof of concept built around a single exploratory question. The non-trivial interaction is the linked selection between the main scatterplot and the detail views, plus dynamic filtering by time, zoning, and TOD threshold. The narrative role is clear: it tests whether TOD is currently serving lower-income places, or whether affordability is lagging behind growth.

  • Data used: MassBuilds project records, MBTA station locations, municipal income aggregates, and municipal context metrics.
  • Interpretation: this view is descriptive, not causal. It shows where income vulnerability, development pressure, and affordability line up.
  • Education and mobility: these are supporting concepts for the final project. Education helps interpret who is capturing neighborhood advantage, and mobility helps test whether transit access improvements are translating into lower daily burden.
  • Main takeaway: TOD becomes anti-gentrification policy only when affordability is built into the growth pattern.
